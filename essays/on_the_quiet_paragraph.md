# On the Quiet Paragraph

Most of the time, when a human and an agent converse,
everything lives in a single running thread. One of them
says something; the other responds; the text piles up in
order. This is a fine way to have a conversation. It is not
a great way to read someone's writing.

When you read a 700-word draft in a chat window, your
reactions are still attached to specific paragraphs — you
know where you started nodding, where you bristled, where
you got lost. But the format flattens that structure. Your
reply goes to the bottom; you paraphrase which paragraph
you meant; the writer tries to reconstruct what you were
reacting to. Friction, lost signal, sometimes a whole
thread derailed by a misunderstood sentence.

## The quiet move

The essay format in this repo makes one small change:
comments anchor to paragraphs.

Not to characters. Not to sentences. Not to the whole
piece. To the paragraph — the unit humans actually form
reactions around. Reading rhythm is paragraph-sized; writing
rhythm usually is too. Pinning a comment to "paragraph 4"
is sharper than "this part in the middle" and looser than
"the second word of the third sentence." The granularity is
the point.

The reader drops a comment on paragraph 4. The writer, when
they come back, sees the comment rendered beside paragraph
4. Neither of them has to remember what it was about.
Neither of them has to summarize. The reaction stayed
attached to the thing it was reacting to, and that's most
of the win.

## Why it changes the feel

Two effects, once you've used it for a while:

First, the reader commits more easily. There's no cost to
flagging one paragraph while enjoying the next one. You're
not breaking flow; you're leaving a mark the writer will
find later. Reading slows down in the right way — you
linger where something catches you, move on where it
doesn't.

Second, the writer can handle specific objections without
rewriting the whole piece. A comment on paragraph 6 is a
local concern; the writer can adjust paragraph 6 or answer
back in the same spot. No cascading revision, no
negotiating which part of the essay was under discussion.

The mechanic is tiny. The ergonomic difference is not.

## When the form works

This works best for:

- Drafts of anything over 300 words where the reader has
  a chance of disagreeing with SOME of it but not all.
- Sketches of decisions where there's a reasoning chain
  and the reviewer wants to push on a specific link.
- Writing that's mostly prose, not mostly structured data.
  Tables and code blocks don't really want paragraph
  comments.

It works less well for:

- Very short pieces (one good paragraph doesn't need
  commenting infrastructure).
- Highly structured documents (specs, reference pages) —
  those want section-level or line-level comments.
- Real-time back-and-forth. The essay format is a slow
  medium; it rewards thinking before responding.

## The form as invitation

There's something else the format does, which is less about
mechanics and more about invitation. A chat asks for
reciprocal response. An essay asks for reading. If you
hand someone a draft with comments enabled, you're
implicitly saying: take your time, react where you react,
don't worry about keeping up with me. That invitation is
hard to extend in a chat window, because the medium is
pushing you toward prompt reply.

Slowing the medium down slows the collaboration down in the
right places. Not everything wants to be faster.
